Finding 1: Performance Correlates, But Weakly

Across all 32 teams and 13 seasons, we found a modest positive correlation between regular season wins and year-over-year follower growth. Teams that improved their win total tended to see above-average follower growth that year. However, the relationship was not strong enough to be predictive — a team could win 12 games and see flat growth, while a 6-win team could experience a major spike.

The correlation was strongest for teams in the middle of the pack — franchises without established massive fan bases where winning served as a reason for casual fans to engage. For the league's largest brands (Dallas Cowboys, New England Patriots), wins and losses had relatively little impact on follower trends year to year.

Finding 2: Cultural Moments Outperform Winning

The single most dramatic follower spikes in our dataset are consistently tied to off-field cultural events rather than wins. The most striking example is the Kansas City Chiefs in 2023: the team gained more followers in that one year than in the previous five years combined, coinciding with Taylor Swift's high-profile attendance at games following her relationship with tight end Travis Kelce.

Similarly, the Tampa Bay Buccaneers saw their Instagram following more than double in 2020–2021, the season Tom Brady signed with the team — despite the team not having significantly changed their social media strategy. The Green Bay Packers saw a notable spike in 2023 tied to gymnast Simone Biles marrying linebacker Jonathan Owens.

KC
Kansas City Chiefs
Won 11 games in both 2022 and 2023 — nearly identical records. Yet their Instagram following grew by roughly 25% in 2022 and over 67% in 2023. The difference? Taylor Swift began attending games in September 2023, generating global media coverage that reached audiences far beyond traditional NFL fans.
DAL
Dallas Cowboys
The Cowboys went 4–12 in 2015 — one of the worst seasons in recent franchise history — yet still ranked among the top three most-followed NFL teams on Instagram that year. Their brand, built over decades as "America's Team," proved largely immune to short-term on-field struggles.
DET
Detroit Lions
The Lions offer the clearest example of performance-driven growth in our dataset. From 2012 to 2021, their follower count barely moved despite modest winning seasons. As the team's culture transformed under coach Dan Campbell and they began competing for championships in 2022–2024, their following grew from 1.8M to 4.2M — nearly tripling in three years. This is the most direct performance-to-followers relationship we found in the data.

Finding 3: Brand Legacy Matters More Than Recent Results

One of the clearest patterns in our data is that franchises with long-established national brand identities — the Cowboys, Patriots, Packers, and 49ers — maintain consistently large and growing audiences regardless of their recent win/loss record. These teams benefit from decades of built-up fan loyalty, merchandise markets, and media presence that insulates their social following from short-term performance swings.

This finding suggests that Instagram follower count is as much a measure of historical brand equity as it is a real-time signal of team quality — an important caveat for anyone trying to use social following as a proxy for team popularity or relevance.

Summary

Key Takeaways
  • Winning does correlate with Instagram growth, but the relationship is weak and inconsistent.
  • Cultural crossover moments — celebrity relationships, viral stories — can produce follower spikes that dwarf anything winning alone can generate.
  • Established brand legacy protects large-market teams from follower loss during bad seasons.
  • Newer or smaller-market franchises show the strongest performance-to-follower correlation, since they lack the brand cushion of legacy teams.
  • Instagram follower count is a poor standalone proxy for team quality or current fan interest.
