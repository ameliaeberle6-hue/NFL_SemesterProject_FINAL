Finding 1: Does Performance Correlate?

SO FAR 04/21: Teams that improved their win total tended to see above-average follower growth that year. However, there where instances where a team could win 12 games and see flat growth, while a 6-win team could experience a major spike. due to this, so far we are not able to give a definitive answer on the correlation between winning and follower growth

Finding 2: Off the field events Outperform Winning

SO FAR 04/21: The single most dramatic follower spikes in our dataset are consistently tied to off-field cultural events rather than wins. The most striking example is the Kansas City Chiefs in 2023: the team gained more followers in that one year than in the previous five years combined, coinciding with Taylor Swift's high-profile attendance at games following her relationship with tight end Travis Kelce.

Similarly, the Tampa Bay Buccaneers saw their Instagram following more than double in 2020–2021, the season Tom Brady signed with the team. The Green Bay Packers saw a notable spike in 2023 tied to gymnast Simone Biles marrying linebacker Jonathan Owens.

Finding 3: Brand Legacy Matters More Than Recent Results

One of the clearest patterns in our data is that franchises with long-established national brand identities maintain consistently large and growing audiences regardless of their recent win/loss record.

Summary

Key Takeaways
  • Cultural crossover moments like celebrity relationships, viral stories can produce follower spikes
  • Established brand legacy protects large-market teams from follower loss during bad seasons.
  • Instagram follower count is a poor standalone proxy for team quality or current fan interest.
