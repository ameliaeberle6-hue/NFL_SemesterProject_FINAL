Section 04

Conclusion

What our project contributes to the conversation about sports, social media, and fan engagement — and the questions it raises for future research.

Does Our Project Answer the Research Question?

Our research question asked: does NFL team performance correlate with Instagram follower growth? The honest answer is: yes, but only partially — and less than you might expect.

We found evidence of a real, positive relationship between winning and follower growth, particularly for smaller-market and mid-tier franchises. When teams like the Detroit Lions or Philadelphia Eagles dramatically improved their on-field product, their Instagram followings grew noticeably faster than teams treading water. In that sense, performance does matter.

But the data also reveals clear limits to this relationship. Established brand-name franchises like the Dallas Cowboys grow their audiences steadily regardless of whether they win or lose. And in several cases — most dramatically the Kansas City Chiefs in 2023 — off-field cultural moments drove follower spikes that no level of on-field performance could plausibly explain on its own. Winning 11 games is not what caused the Chiefs to gain nearly 6 million Instagram followers in a single year.

What This Contributes

This project contributes a longitudinal, league-wide perspective that is largely missing from the existing literature. Most prior studies examine a single team, a single season, or a single platform. By tracking all 32 franchises over 13 years, we are able to identify patterns that only emerge at scale: the brand-equity effect for legacy franchises, the ceiling on performance-driven growth, and the outsized role of cultural crossover moments.

Our interactive visualizations also make this data accessible and explorable in ways that a static paper cannot. Readers can compare any two teams, zoom in on specific seasons, and see exactly where cultural events align with follower spikes — letting the data tell its own story rather than relying solely on our interpretation.

New Questions Raised

Questions for Future Research
  • → Does the Taylor Swift effect persist? Do followers gained through celebrity association remain engaged fans, or do they unfollow when the cultural moment fades?
  • → How does this pattern differ across other social platforms — does TikTok show stronger or weaker correlations with performance compared to Instagram?
  • → Do teams with larger Instagram followings generate more revenue? Does follower count actually translate to ticket sales, merchandise, and TV ratings?
  • → How do individual player accounts factor in? If a star player leaves a team, does the team's follower count suffer, and by how much?
  • → Is there a threshold winning percentage below which teams begin to actively lose followers — or do even the worst teams hold steady due to diehard loyalty?

Final Reflection

What surprised us most in this project was not that cultural moments matter — we suspected that going in — but how much they matter relative to winning. The NFL is the most-watched sports league in the United States, and its teams compete fiercely for media attention. In that environment, attaching a franchise to a cultural phenomenon like Taylor Swift or Simone Biles can be worth more Instagram followers than a Super Bowl run.

This has real implications for how teams think about branding, community engagement, and the definition of success beyond the scoreboard. A team that wins consistently but remains culturally insular will likely be outpaced on social media by a team that connects — even accidentally — with a broader cultural moment.

Social media following is not a perfect measure of fan loyalty or team quality. But as a window into how sports brands intersect with popular culture, it turns out to be a surprisingly rich and telling dataset.
