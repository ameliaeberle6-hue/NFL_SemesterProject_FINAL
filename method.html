Research
Methodology

A step-by-step account of how we collected, organized, and analyzed data to answer our research question: does NFL team performance correlate with Instagram follower growth?

Research Question

Our central question is: Does NFL team performance correlate with Instagram follower growth? This is a quantitative research question — it asks whether two measurable variables move together in a statistically meaningful way. To answer it, we needed two parallel datasets: one tracking each team's regular season win/loss record year by year, and one tracking each team's Instagram follower count over the same period.

Data Collection

  • 01
    NFL Win/Loss Records — Covers.com We collected regular season win totals for all 32 NFL teams from 2012 through 2024 using Covers.com's historical standings pages (e.g., covers.com/sport/football/nfl/standings/2012-2013). For each season, we recorded each team's regular season win total (out of 16 games for 2012–2020, and 17 games from 2021 onward). Data was recorded manually into a shared spreadsheet.
  • 02
    Instagram Follower Counts — Social Blade Instagram follower counts were collected using Social Blade (socialblade.com), a third-party analytics platform that tracks historical social media statistics. For each of the 32 NFL teams, we looked up the official team Instagram account and recorded approximate follower counts at the end of each calendar year from 2012 to 2024. Where exact end-of-year figures were unavailable, we used the closest available data point and noted this in our records.
  • 03
    Cultural Event Annotation In addition to our two primary variables, we manually compiled a list of notable off-field events that could plausibly explain outlier follower spikes — including high-profile player signings, celebrity relationships with players, Super Bowl wins, and viral media moments. These were not used in our quantitative analysis but are flagged in our visualizations to provide qualitative context.

Scope & Limitations

To keep the project manageable, we made several deliberate scoping decisions. First, we focused only on regular season win totals rather than more granular performance metrics (e.g., points scored, margin of victory, playoff outcomes). Second, we used year-end follower snapshots rather than tracking follower counts on a weekly or monthly basis. Third, because Social Blade's historical data has some gaps for accounts prior to 2015, follower counts for the earliest years in our dataset carry more uncertainty than later years.

We also limited our analysis to the official team Instagram accounts, excluding player accounts, league accounts, and fan pages. This was necessary to ensure we were measuring team-level brand growth rather than individual athlete popularity.

Analysis Approach

Our analysis is primarily quantitative — we compared win totals to year-over-year follower growth rates for each team and looked for patterns across the league. We also conducted a more qualitative layer of analysis by identifying teams whose follower growth significantly outpaced or underperformed their win/loss record, then researching what off-field factors might explain those anomalies.

Findings are presented through interactive line graphs and bar charts built using Chart.js, allowing readers to explore individual team trends and compare teams side by side. Gold-highlighted data points on the charts indicate years with confirmed cultural or media events that may have influenced follower counts.

AI Tool Usage

Reflection on AI in Our Process

We used two AI tools over the course of this project: ChatGPT (OpenAI) and Claude (Anthropic). It is important to be transparent about what we delegated to these tools and what we chose to do ourselves.

Claude was used extensively to build the website you are currently reading. We described the structure we wanted — a multi-page site with interactive charts, a pink color scheme, NFL team colors, and the official NFL logo — and Claude generated the HTML, CSS, and JavaScript code. We then reviewed the output, tested it in a browser, and requested revisions iteratively. We made deliberate choices about what to ask for versus what to specify ourselves: for example, we chose the color scheme and overall structure, while Claude handled the technical implementation of Chart.js and the responsive layout.

ChatGPT was used during the early research and brainstorming phase to help us identify relevant academic literature on sports and social media and to think through how to frame our research question. We did not use it to generate data or write final prose that appears in this site.

Using these tools raised important questions for us about authorship, delegation, and learning. Building the website with Claude was faster than learning web development from scratch, but it also meant we sometimes received code we did not fully understand. This required us to ask follow-up questions, test carefully, and develop enough literacy to evaluate the output critically. The data collection, analysis, and written content are entirely our own work. The AI tools served as a technical assistant for implementation — not a replacement for research or original thinking.
