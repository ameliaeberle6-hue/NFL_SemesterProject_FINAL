Section 01

Literature
Review

A survey of research on social media, sports fandom, and the relationship between athletic performance and digital audience growth.

Social Media & Sports Organizations

Scholars have increasingly studied how professional sports franchises use social media to cultivate and expand their fan bases. Pegoraro (2010) was among the first to examine how professional athletes use Twitter to communicate directly with fans, finding that authentic, personal content generated significantly more engagement than promotional material. This early work established a foundational principle still relevant to Instagram today: audiences respond to personality and access, not just performance.

Building on this, Filo, Lock, and Karg (2015) conducted a systematic review of social media use in sport, concluding that platforms serve three primary functions for sports organizations: relationship building, information sharing, and community formation. Crucially, their analysis found that teams with consistent social media strategies maintained stronger follower bases even during losing seasons — suggesting that off-field content strategy can buffer against on-field underperformance.

Key Finding from the Literature

Teams with strong brand identities and consistent social media content strategies tend to retain — and even grow — their audiences during losing seasons. Winning helps, but it is not the only driver of follower growth.

Athletic Performance & Fan Engagement

Several studies have examined the direct relationship between on-field success and fan behavior. Swanson, Gwinner, Larson, and Janda (2003) found that winning percentage is positively correlated with game attendance, merchandise sales, and media consumption. However, their findings also showed that this effect plateaus — teams that are consistently good do not see proportional increases in engagement over time. Novelty and momentum matter as much as raw winning percentage.

More recently, Watanabe, Yan, and Soebbing (2015) studied Twitter activity around NFL games and found that game outcomes — particularly unexpected wins or losses — generated far more social media activity than anticipated results. This suggests that surprise and narrative drama are stronger engagement drivers than simple winning, which has direct implications for understanding Instagram follower trends.

Celebrity Culture & Sports Crossover

An important and underexplored dimension of sports social media growth is the role of celebrity culture and media crossover. Andrews and Jackson (2001) introduced the concept of the "sport celebrity" as a cultural commodity — arguing that certain athletes transcend their sport to become broader pop-culture figures, dramatically expanding their audience beyond traditional sports fans.

This phenomenon has become even more pronounced in the social media era. Geurin-Eagleman and Burch (2016) found that Instagram accounts belonging to athletes who engaged with entertainment, fashion, and lifestyle content attracted significantly larger and more diverse audiences than those focused solely on athletic content. This has direct relevance to our study: teams associated with high-profile celebrities — whether through player relationships or cultural partnerships — may experience follower spikes that have little to do with their win/loss record.

Gap in the Literature

While existing research examines either performance-based engagement or celebrity-driven growth in isolation, few studies directly compare these two forces across an entire league over multiple seasons. Our project addresses this gap by tracking all 32 NFL teams simultaneously from 2012 to 2024.

Instagram as a Research Subject

Instagram, launched in 2010 and acquired by Facebook (now Meta) in 2012, has become one of the primary platforms for sports content. Hambrick and Mahoney (2011) noted that visual platforms are particularly well-suited to sports content because they capture the aesthetic and emotional dimensions of athletic performance in ways that text-based media cannot.

Ruihley and Runyan (2016) specifically studied how NFL teams used Instagram during the 2014 season, finding that teams that posted more frequently and used more varied content types — behind-the-scenes footage, player spotlights, game highlights — earned higher follower growth rates than teams that posted only game-day content. This suggests that content strategy mediates the relationship between performance and audience growth, complicating any simple correlation between winning and follower counts.

What We Still Don't Know

Despite a growing body of research, several questions remain underexplored. Most studies focus on short time windows — a single season or a few years — making it difficult to identify long-term trends. Additionally, few studies have attempted to isolate the specific contribution of external cultural events (celebrity news, viral moments, off-field controversies) from performance-based growth. Our project contributes to addressing both of these gaps by examining a thirteen-year longitudinal dataset and flagging known cultural events as variables in our analysis.
